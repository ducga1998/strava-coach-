# Strava AI Coach — The Coaching Vision

*Written from the perspective of an ultra/trail running coach.*
*Target athlete: You. Training for VMM 160km.*

---

## The Problem With How Runners Train Today

You finish a run. Garmin syncs. Strava shows you a number — distance, pace, heart rate. You look at it for 10 seconds, tap the kudos button on a friend's activity, and put your phone down.

Nothing changed. You learned nothing. Tomorrow you'll go out and do roughly the same thing you did today, because nobody told you otherwise.

That is not training. That is exercising with expensive GPS.

A real coach doesn't look at your average pace. A real coach looks at what happened to your heart rate in the last 30 minutes of that run. Whether your cadence dropped on the third climb. Whether you're digging yourself into a hole that will cost you three weeks of fitness in month 4. Whether the way you're descending right now is going to destroy your quads at kilometer 90 of VMM.

That's what this tool does. It's the coach sitting next to you reading your data, not the app congratulating you for finishing.

---

## Who This Is For

You are 30–45 years old. You run ultras or trail races — VMM, UTMB, UTSN, Dalat Ultra Sky Race. You're self-coached because you can't afford a human coach, or because you haven't found one who understands trail and mountain running well enough to be worth the money.

You have data. Lots of it. Every run is on Strava, your Garmin tracks heart rate, elevation, cadence. But the data just sits there. Strava tells you your suffer score. TrainingPeaks costs $20/month and takes 6 hours to understand. You mostly just train by feel, which means you mostly just train too hard on easy days and not hard enough on hard days.

Your real problem is not motivation. It's interpretation. You don't know what your data is telling you.

---

## The Coaching Philosophy

**Rule 1: Every statement must have a number.**
"Your aerobic base is improving" means nothing. "Your heart rate at 5:30/km pace dropped from 158 to 151 over 6 weeks" means everything.

**Rule 2: One insight per run, not ten.**
A coach who gives you 10 things to fix after every session is not a coach — they're noise. The best coaches identify the single most important thing and say it clearly. Everything else can wait.

**Rule 3: The next session matters more than this one.**
The debrief is not a grade. It's a signal for what comes next. The question is never "how did I do?" — it's "what does this tell me about tomorrow?"

**Rule 4: Terrain changes everything.**
A 5:30/km pace on flat road is Zone 2. The same pace on a 12% grade is Zone 4. If your coaching tool doesn't understand elevation, it doesn't understand trail running. Grade-adjusted effort is the only honest unit for mountain runners.

**Rule 5: Volume kills before speed does.**
Most ultra runners don't get injured from running fast. They get injured from adding too much volume, too fast, after a motivated week. The job of a coach is to be the boring voice of restraint when you feel great and want to run 120km in a week for the first time.

## What a Coach Looks at After Each Run

These are the signals that matter. Not all of them matter for every run — the coach identifies which one is the story of that particular session.

### 1. Cardiac Drift — The Honesty Metric

Your heart rate should stay roughly flat if your effort is sustainable. If you start a 2-hour run at 145 bpm and finish it at 165 bpm at the same pace, something happened. Either you went out too hard, you're dehydrated, you're fatigued from the week, or your aerobic system is not efficient enough for the distance yet.

Cardiac drift above 5% on a run that was supposed to be easy is a red flag. Above 8% means you weren't running easy — you were just running slowly.

A good debrief: *"HR drifted 7.2% in the final 40 minutes. At your current training load (ACWR 1.4) this likely reflects accumulated fatigue from the last 5 days (TSB -18). Prioritize sleep tonight and keep tomorrow below Zone 2."*

A bad debrief: *"Great effort today! Your heart rate data shows you worked hard."*

### 2. Aerobic Decoupling — Are You Building or Just Grinding?

Decoupling measures whether your pace is getting harder to maintain as the run goes on, relative to heart rate. Think of it as efficiency decay.

Less than 5% decoupling: your aerobic system held up. You were in control.
5–8%: you were pushing the limit of your current aerobic capacity for that duration.
Above 8%: the run was longer or harder than your current base can support cleanly.

For a 160km race, you need to run 24–36 hours. Your aerobic system must be almost bulletproof. Chronic decoupling above 6% on long runs means your base is not deep enough yet. More Zone 2 time, less racing in training.

### 3. Descent Heart Rate — The VMM-Specific Signal

This one most runners never hear about, and it's the most important one for VMM 160km.

On a descent, your heart rate should drop or stay flat. You're going downhill — cardiovascular demand should be lower. If your HR spikes on descents, or stays the same as it was on the climb, your quads are working too hard as brakes. Your running economy on downhills is poor.

At VMM, you will descend over 8,000 meters total across 160km. Runners who haven't trained their descents arrive at km 80 with destroyed quadriceps and spend the second half of the race walking. This is the single most common reason for DNFs in the 100–160km range.

*"Your HR averaged 158 bpm on the 4km descent from Mang Yang Pass — only 3 bpm lower than your climb HR. This means your quads are absorbing shock instead of releasing it. Add 2× downhill repeats per week: 10–15 min continuous descent at controlled effort, focus on relaxed leg turnover not braking."*

That sentence could save someone's VMM.

### 4. Training Load — Your Compass

**ACWR (Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio)** is the most important number a self-coached runner can know. It compares what you've done in the last 7 days against your average over the last 28 days.

- **0.8 – 1.3**: Green zone. You're loading appropriately.
- **1.3 – 1.5**: Yellow. You're pushing your current capacity. One hard week is fine. Two in a row starts accumulating real injury risk.
- **Above 1.5**: Red. This is where ITB, stress fractures, and Achilles problems happen. Not from a single run — from repeated exposure to this ratio.

For VMM preparation, you'll need to hit peak weeks of 100–120km. If your body is currently used to 60km weeks, jumping to 100km is an ACWR of 1.67. That's the injury zone, no matter how good your legs feel on day 3.

The coach's job is to build you toward those peak weeks over 16–20 weeks so your chronic load rises gradually and the ratio stays green throughout.

**CTL, ATL, TSB** (Fitness, Fatigue, Form) tell you where you are in the cycle:
- High CTL = you've built real fitness over months
- High ATL = you're currently carrying fatigue
- TSB = CTL minus ATL = your form today

Going into VMM, you want CTL as high as possible (built over months), ATL falling (you've tapered), and TSB between +5 and +15 on race day. Not +30 (undertapered, detrained) and not -15 (still carrying fatigue).

### 5. Zone Distribution — Are You Training the Way You Think?

Most runners think they're doing Zone 2. They're usually doing Zone 3.

Zone 3 is not recovery. It's not aerobic base building. It's the "comfortable hard" that feels productive but does the least of any zone for building ultra endurance. It's also the zone that accumulates the most wear without enough adaptation signal.

Polarized training — the approach used by most elite ultra runners — means approximately:
- 80% of runs at true Zone 1–2 (conversational, never breathless)
- 20% of runs at Zone 4–5 (genuinely hard tempo or intervals)
- Almost zero time at Zone 3

If a runner's long runs show 35% Zone 3, the coach will flag it: *"55% of this run was Zone 1-2, but 35% was Zone 3 — likely on uphills where pace stayed the same and HR drifted up. On your next long run, walk any section where HR exceeds 150 bpm (Zone 3 for you). The goal is keeping the door below your lactate threshold, not just running slow on flat."*

### 6. Cadence — The Quiet Fatigue Signal

Cadence (steps per minute) is a proxy for neuromuscular freshness. A rested runner holds their cadence. A fatigued runner shuffles.

Normal cadence for trail running: 160–175 spm depending on terrain and pace. A drop of more than 5–8 spm from your personal norm, without a corresponding increase in technical terrain difficulty, is a fatigue signal that heart rate won't catch.

*"Cadence dropped from 168 spm (first hour) to 158 spm (final hour) on a section where the terrain didn't get significantly harder. This 6% drop is consistent with neuromuscular fatigue — you were still aerobically capable but your legs were losing spring. Consider a shorter long run next week."*

## The Post-Run Debrief — What It Must Look Like

Every run generates a three-part debrief. Not a grade. Not encouragement. A diagnostic.

### Part 1: The Verdict
One sentence on load status — was this run appropriate given your current training phase and accumulated fatigue?

Examples:
- *"TSS 78, 30% above your 28-day average. ACWR now 1.38 — top of green zone. This was a productive overreach if the next 2 days are recovery."*
- *"Effort was appropriate (hrTSS 54, right at your weekly median). ACWR 0.95 — you're in a deload pattern. If VMM is 9 weeks away, this week is correct."*
- *"TSS 112, 85% above your 28-day average. ACWR jumped to 1.6 — red zone. The run felt good because you were fresh from yesterday's rest day, not because the load was sustainable. One more day like this without easy recovery and injury risk is real."*

### Part 2: The Technical Insight
One finding from the run's physiology data. The most important signal, not a list.

Examples:
- *"HR drift 8.4% in the final 35 minutes. For a run this duration, acceptable drift is under 5%. You either went out 8–10 seconds/km too fast or your aerobic capacity for 2.5-hour efforts isn't there yet."*
- *"Descent HR averaged 162 bpm — higher than your climb HR (158 bpm). This is a descending efficiency problem, not a fitness problem. Your quads are acting as brakes. Fix: 2× weekly downhill strides on any 6–10% grade, short (30 seconds), focus on relaxed ankle strike, high cadence."*
- *"Zone distribution: 31% Z3, 48% Z2, 21% Z1. You spent nearly a third of a 'recovery' run in moderate-hard effort. This erases the recovery purpose entirely. On your next easy day: cap HR at 140 bpm and walk every climb without exception."*

### Part 3: Next Step
One concrete instruction for the next session. Not a philosophy. A workout.

Examples:
- *"Tomorrow: full rest or 30-min walk only. Sleep 8+ hours. You've run 5 days straight with cumulative TSS 290 — your body needs synthesis time, not more stimulus."*
- *"Next long run (Saturday): cap at 2.5 hours maximum. Primary goal is aerobic time below 148 bpm. If HR exceeds 148 on uphills, walk. No exceptions. This is a base-building run, not a suffer-fest."*
- *"This week: add one downhill-specific session. Find a trail with 200–300m of continuous descent. Run down 4–5 times at controlled effort, cadence above 165, HR allowed to drop naturally. No hard uphill efforts same day."*

## VMM 160km — The Specific Coaching Context

VMM 160km is not a race you run by pace. It's a race you manage by terrain and effort. The following coaching principles are specific to this event.

**The race is decided on the descents, not the climbs.**
Most runners know to manage effort on uphills. The elite secret is managing legs on downhills. Every 1,000m of descent at high speed costs more quad fiber than 3,000m of climbing. Your training should reflect this.

**Volume without terrain is incomplete preparation.**
Running 100km flat per week does not prepare your legs for VMM. 80km with 5,000m D+ is closer to race-specific stress. The AI coach needs to see both your weekly kilometers and your weekly elevation, and weight them appropriately.

**ACWR is your injury compass from months 3 to 6 of build.**
When you hit the phase where weekly volume goes from 70km to 100km, ACWR becomes critical. Most VMM DNFs that are labeled "injury" are really the consequence of an ACWR above 1.5 sustained for 3–4 weeks because the athlete felt good and the coach (or the athlete playing coach) didn't pull back.

**Taper must start 3 weeks out, not 1.**
Most self-coached runners taper one week and show up carrying fatigue. For a 160km race, TSB on race day should be +8 to +15. That requires reducing ATL starting 3 weeks out, with no panic return to volume if you feel flat in week 2 of taper (that flatness is normal, it means ATL is falling).

**Phase markers:**
- 20+ weeks out: Base phase. Build aerobic capacity. Zero race-specific work. Long runs at conversation pace. Weekly mileage increases 8–10% max.
- 12–20 weeks out: Build phase. Introduce terrain. Back-to-back long runs on weekends. Begin descent training.
- 6–12 weeks out: Peak phase. Highest volume. One race-simulation effort (50–80km with full elevation). ACWR managed very carefully.
- 0–6 weeks out: Taper. Volume falls, intensity briefly rises then falls. TSB rises toward race day.

## What This Tool Is Not

It is not a training plan generator. It does not tell you what to do on every day of the week. It does not replace periodization.

What it does is read the run you just finished and tell you the one thing a coach would tell you — the thing Strava never will.

It is the voice that says: *"You're overreaching and you feel great right now, which is exactly when people get hurt. Tomorrow is a walk."*

Or: *"Your descending HR is a problem and VMM will punish you for it. Here's the specific drill to fix it."*

Or: *"Three months ago this run would have been hard. Today your heart rate was 8 bpm lower at the same pace. That's what base-building looks like. Keep going."*

That's the product. Not a dashboard. A coach.

## What "Good" Looks Like at Month 3

A runner has been using the tool for 12 weeks preparing for VMM. They open the app after their Saturday long run. In two taps they see:

> **Verdict:** TSS 94 — right at your Build phase target. ACWR 1.18, deep in green zone. You've run 6 consecutive weeks of progressive load without exceeding 1.3. That's professional-level restraint.
>
> **Insight:** HR drift was 4.1% (under your 5% threshold) for a 3h10m run. Eight weeks ago this run would have caused 9–10% drift. Your aerobic base is measurably deeper. Efficiency at 6:10/km trail pace has improved.
>
> **Next Session:** Sunday easy run, 60–75 min maximum, full Zone 1-2 (HR under 145). Monday rest. The key load came today — Sunday is recovery, not bonus training.

That runner doesn't need a coach on the phone. They have the signal they need.
